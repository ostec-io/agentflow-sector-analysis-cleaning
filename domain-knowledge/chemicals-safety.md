## 1. Book Summary

Across the HSE, EPA Safer Choice, EPA Safer Ingredients, and Green Seal documents, the core message is that chemical safety in cleaning is a systems problem, not just a product-label problem. The HSE material focuses on COSHH duties, real exposure pathways, wet-work dermatitis, glove/PPE limitations, LEV management, and the need for supervision, records, and health surveillance. The EPA and Green Seal materials add a second layer: ingredient-level hazard screening, packaging restrictions, multilingual labeling, product training, disclosure, auditability, and service-certification mechanics. Taken together, the corpus describes a market where cleaning companies, formulators, and service providers need continuous orchestration across procurement, risk assessment, worker training, frontline execution, incident response, and certification evidence.

## 2. Domain Problems and Pain Points

### P1. COSHH assessments are often disconnected from how work is really done

- **Problem**: Employers are required to assess risk, but the real exposure picture depends on tasks, frequency, who does the work, and where contamination actually travels.
- **Who feels it**: Cleaning supervisors, EHS managers, frontline cleaners, contractors, visitors, auditors.
- **Current approach**: Manual COSHH forms, supervisor observation, SDS review, and occasional consultant support.
- **Chapter/section reference**: HSE-COSHH-Brief-Guide, “Assessing risk,” Page 4; HSE-Managing-Skin-Exposure-Risks, “How do I assess the risks?” and “Exposure pathways,” Pages 9-10.

### P2. Chemical handling, decanting, storage, and mixing create preventable incidents

- **Problem**: Splashing, open-container carrying, decanting into unsuitable containers, and mixing bleach with acidic or ammonia-based cleaners can cause burns, harmful gases, or accidental ingestion risks.
- **Who feels it**: Frontline cleaners, hospitality workers, supervisors, first-aiders, customers nearby.
- **Current approach**: Basic training, label reliance, ad hoc spill procedures, manual storage rules.
- **Chapter/section reference**: HSE-Cleaning-Substances-Hospitality-Guide, “What are the health risks?” and “Guidance for employers/employees,” Pages 1-3.

### P3. PPE is routinely treated as the control, even though it is supposed to be the last resort

- **Problem**: The sources repeatedly stress elimination, substitution, enclosure, extraction, and work redesign before PPE, but many workplaces jump straight to gloves or masks.
- **Who feels it**: Employers, workers, regulators, occupational hygienists.
- **Current approach**: Issue PPE, add a short briefing, rely on worker compliance.
- **Chapter/section reference**: HSE-COSHH-Brief-Guide, “Choosing control measures,” Pages 5-6; HSE-PPE-at-Work-L25, Regulation 4 guidance, Pages 10-12.

### P4. Glove and PPE selection is technically difficult and errors increase exposure

- **Problem**: No glove protects against everything; permeation, degradation, fit, comfort, contamination inside the glove, allergy risk, and task dexterity all matter.
- **Who feels it**: Procurement teams, safety managers, cleaners, maintenance teams, suppliers.
- **Current approach**: Supplier charts, trial and error, generic glove issuance, infrequent retraining.
- **Chapter/section reference**: HSE-Selecting-Chemical-Protective-Gloves, Pages 2-3; HSE-Managing-Skin-Exposure-Risks, “Choosing and using protective gloves,” Pages 15-18; HSE-PPE-at-Work-L25, “Hand and arm protection,” Pages 24-25.

### P5. Wet work and dermatitis are under-monitored until they become serious

- **Problem**: Repeated handwashing, prolonged glove wear, immersion, and frequent water contact can damage the skin barrier long before a business reacts.
- **Who feels it**: Cleaners, caterers, healthcare workers, hairdressers, occupational health teams, employers.
- **Current approach**: Workers self-report late, supervisors notice visible symptoms, occasional occupational health referrals.
- **Chapter/section reference**: HSE-Preventing-Contact-Dermatitis-and-Urticaria, Pages 1-3; HSE-Managing-Skin-Exposure-Risks, “The risks from wet work,” “Skincare,” and “Check for early signs of skin disease,” Pages 10 and 19-23.

### P6. Worker instructions are often too text-heavy, too technical, or not accessible enough

- **Problem**: SDSs are difficult to use on the job, and labels or training often fail non-English-speaking or low-literacy workers.
- **Who feels it**: Frontline workers, supervisors, trainers, service providers, auditors.
- **Current approach**: Hand out SDSs, short induction training, posters, informal verbal instructions.
- **Chapter/section reference**: HSE-COSHH-Brief-Guide, “Training, instruction and information,” Page 7; HSE-Cleaning-Substances-Hospitality-Guide, Page 2; GreenSeal-GS37, “4.3.1 Training” and “4.3.2 Label Language,” Page 14.

### P7. LEV systems fail because commissioning, testing, and logbook discipline break down

- **Problem**: LEV only protects workers if the hood, airflow, user manual, commissioning report, logbook, daily checks, repairs, and statutory re-tests all happen.
- **Who feels it**: Facilities teams, safety managers, engineers, cleaning and maintenance workers, inspectors.
- **Current approach**: Contractor installation, periodic servicing, paper logbooks, reactive repairs.
- **Chapter/section reference**: HSE-Clearing-the-Air-LEV, Pages 3-8; HSE-COSHH-Brief-Guide, Page 6.

### P8. Safer-chemistry screening is too data-heavy for most buyers and formulators to do well

- **Problem**: EPA and Green Seal require review across acute toxicity, carcinogenicity, mutagenicity, reproductive toxicity, sensitization, biodegradability, bioaccumulation, aquatic toxicity, VOCs, phosphorus, packaging chemicals, and special ingredient classes.
- **Who feels it**: Product formulators, procurement teams, private-label brands, auditors, third-party profilers.
- **Current approach**: Spreadsheet comparisons, supplier questionnaires, consultant toxicology review, fragmented registry searches.
- **Chapter/section reference**: EPA-Safer-Ingredients-Master-Criteria-2024, Sections 5.1-5.10, Pages 9-23; GreenSeal-GS37, Sections 2.1-2.2 and 3.0, Pages 7-13; EPA-Safer-Choice-Standard-2024, Sections 5.2-5.18, Pages 31-35.

### P9. Packaging and labeling compliance is a separate burden, not a side note

- **Problem**: Recyclability, post-consumer recycled content, resin codes, PFAS/BPA/phthalate bans, heavy-metal limits, dilution directions, disposal instructions, and fragrance declarations all need verification.
- **Who feels it**: Product managers, packaging teams, sustainability teams, regulatory teams, private-label customers.
- **Current approach**: Packaging supplier declarations, manual artwork reviews, certification checklists.
- **Chapter/section reference**: GreenSeal-GS37, Section 3.0 and 4.3, Pages 12-15; EPA-Safer-Choice-Standard-2024, “4.2.5 Primary packaging,” Pages 24-25.

### P10. Disclosure, audit, and evidence trails are document-heavy and brittle

- **Problem**: Certification depends on formula disclosure, supplier data, residual reporting, bills of material, batch tickets, certificates of analysis, labels, audit responses, and corrective-action plans.
- **Who feels it**: Manufacturers, profilers, compliance teams, auditors, EPA/Green Seal partners.
- **Current approach**: Email, spreadsheets, document portals, periodic desk audits and site audits.
- **Chapter/section reference**: EPA-Safer-Choice-Standard-2024, “3.2 Information and Formulation Requirements,” “3.6 Verification of Partnership Compliance,” “3.8 Ingredient Communication,” and Annex C, Pages 16-21 and 64.

### P11. Cleaning-service certification is governed by procurement evidence and exception handling

- **Problem**: Service providers must prove they use Safer Choice-certified cleaners and DfE-certified disinfectants to the maximum extent practicable, while documenting justified exceptions.
- **Who feels it**: Cleaning service providers, facility managers, procurement teams, profilers.
- **Current approach**: Annual invoice collection, attestations, manual exception narratives, remote audits.
- **Chapter/section reference**: EPA-Safer-Choice-Standard-2024, Sections 7.3-7.5, Pages 39-41.

### P12. Special ingredient classes create edge-case compliance traps

- **Problem**: Fragrances, enzymes, microorganisms, wipes, pet products, direct-release products, and dermal-contact products all carry extra restrictions that can be missed in standard review flows.
- **Who feels it**: Formulators, regulatory teams, private-label brands, service buyers.
- **Current approach**: Specialist consultant review and manual exception tracking.
- **Chapter/section reference**: GreenSeal-GS37, Annex D and Annex E, Pages 23-25; EPA-Safer-Choice-Standard-2024, Sections 4.7-4.8 and 5.5, 5.10, 5.11, Pages 27-35.

## 3. Product Ideas for AgentFlow

### 1. COSHH Flow

- **Product name**: COSHH Flow
- **One-liner**: A task-aware COSHH operating system that turns SDSs, work observations, and control hierarchies into approved, reviewable control plans.
- **The problem it solves**: P1, P2, P3, P6, P10.
- **How AgentFlow powers it**: Multi-agent orchestration to parallelize SDS parsing, task mapping, exposure-route analysis, and control drafting; BPMN process definitions for assessment, supervisor review, worker consultation, approval, training, and periodic re-review; human-in-the-loop approvals for high-risk tasks; scheduled automation for annual reviews and change-triggered reassessments; audit trails and provenance for every control decision; MCP integrations to SDS repositories, chemical inventories, HR rosters, and LMS tools.
- **Key agents needed**: SDS parser agent, exposure-pathway agent, control-hierarchy agent, training-content agent, compliance reviewer agent.
- **Integration points**: SDS libraries, chemical inventory systems, HR systems, LMS platforms, incident-reporting tools, document storage.
- **Feasibility**: High — the rules are explicit, the workflow is document-heavy, and the approvals map cleanly to AgentFlow.

### 2. GloveFit

- **Product name**: GloveFit
- **One-liner**: A glove and PPE decision engine that matches chemicals, contact type, duration, dexterity, allergies, and fit requirements to the safest practical kit.
- **The problem it solves**: P3, P4, P6.
- **How AgentFlow powers it**: Multi-agent orchestration to compare glove charts, task conditions, EN standard requirements, and wearer constraints; knowledge graphs to retain glove-performance history by chemical, task, and site; scheduled automation for replacement intervals and refresher training; event-driven triggers from defect reports; pair sessions and handoffs between safety and procurement agents when no suitable glove is available.
- **Key agents needed**: PPE selector agent, glove-material analyst, fit-and-sizing agent, procurement agent, training agent.
- **Integration points**: Vendor glove databases, QR-coded issue logs, incident tools, purchasing systems, staff medical restriction data where permitted.
- **Feasibility**: Medium — highly buildable, but vendor breakthrough/degradation data quality is uneven and often proprietary.

## 4. Workflow Patterns

### Workflow 1. COSHH assessment to approval

- **Workflow name**: COSHH assessment to approval
- **Steps involved**: Ingest SDS and task description; identify exposure routes and affected worker groups; compare elimination/substitution/engineering options; draft controls and PPE; review with supervisor and workers; approve; train; schedule review.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Parallel task sprint for SDS parsing, exposure analysis, and control drafting; BPMN process with supervisor approval and worker consultation gates; cron-based reassessment; knowledge graph for site/task reuse.

### Workflow 2. Safer chemical purchasing and substitution

- **Workflow name**: Safer chemical purchasing and substitution
- **Steps involved**: Raise need for a product; screen candidate products against EPA/Green Seal rules; compare performance, VOC, packaging, fragrance, and PPE implications; approve pilot; monitor incidents and worker feedback; standardize or reject.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Event trigger from procurement request; multi-agent comparison pipeline; human-in-the-loop approval for exceptions; cross-project memory of previous ingredient or product decisions.

### Workflow 3. Wet-work surveillance and early intervention

- **Workflow name**: Wet-work surveillance and early intervention
- **Steps involved**: Log task frequency and hand-wash exposure; collect symptom prompts; trigger responsible-person skin checks; refer to occupational health if needed; adjust controls, gloves, cleansers, and moisturizers; verify improvement.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Recurring scheduled tasks; Sentinel anomaly detection on symptom clusters; BPMN case flow for referral and follow-up; audit trail for surveillance records.

### Workflow 4. Glove and PPE lifecycle management

- **Workflow name**: Glove and PPE lifecycle management
- **Steps involved**: Identify chemical/task combination; select glove and other PPE; fit and issue; train on donning/doffing; inspect and replace; log defects and worker complaints; reassess for allergy, comfort, or exposure failures.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Multi-agent recommendation sprint; approval gate for non-standard substitutions; event-driven defect reporting; handoff between safety, stores, and procurement agents.

### Workflow 5. LEV commissioning, testing, and fail escalation

- **Workflow name**: LEV commissioning, testing, and fail escalation
- **Steps involved**: Specify LEV need; select supplier; commission and record benchmarks; run daily/weekly checks; schedule thorough examination and test; if failed, stop work or add temporary protection; repair; recommission; resume.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: BPMN process with timer events, fail signals, and reopening approvals; scheduled tasks for logbook checks; provenance tracking for commissioning reports and action plans.

### Workflow 6. Spill and incompatibility response

- **Workflow name**: Spill and incompatibility response
- **Steps involved**: Report incident; identify substance and route of exposure; isolate area; select PPE and cleanup procedure; execute first aid; document root cause; update controls and training.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Event-driven trigger; concurrent agents for chemical lookup, first aid, and escalation; CAPA follow-up sprint; Sentinel alerts for repeated failures.

### Workflow 7. Cleaning-service certification and annual audit

- **Workflow name**: Cleaning-service certification and annual audit
- **Steps involved**: Gather invoices and attestations; match products to Safer Choice/DfE listings; document exceptions; review employee information materials; submit audit pack; correct noncompliance; renew.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Annual cron workflow; document-review sprint; human review for exceptions; audit evidence bucket with provenance; renewal approvals.

## 5. Standards, APIs and Data Sources

### Industry standards and frameworks

- COSHH Regulations 2002 (as amended).
- Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 1992 (as amended).
- REACH.
- GHS classification and labeling criteria.
- Workplace Exposure Limits (EH40).
- EN 374-2 and EN 374-3 for protective gloves against water/chemicals.
- EN 388, EN 407, EN 421, and EN 511 glove standards.
- ASSE 1055B backflow prevention for closed dilution-control systems.
- ISO/IEC 17065 for certification bodies and profilers.
- ISO 8317 and EN 862 for child-resistant packaging.
- ASTM D4488, ASTM D5343, HCPA DCC methods, AATCC 171, CRI TM-110, WoolSafe.
- CARB Method 310 for VOC content.
- GREENGUARD Gold dynamic chamber method for inhalation/emissions testing.
- TPCH model legislation for toxics in packaging.
- OECD and OPPTS test guidelines cited in EPA Safer Choice and EPA Safer Ingredients.

### APIs and data exchange formats

- No formal web APIs are named in the corpus; the ecosystem is primarily document- and registry-driven.
- Safety Data Sheets (SDS) and technical data sheets.
- Product labels, dilution directions, disposal directions, and multilingual/icon-based instructions.
- Bills of material, batch tickets, certificates of analysis, bills of lading, and purchase invoices.
- CAS numbers and standardized chemical naming systems such as CAS, INCI, IUPAC, and HCPA nomenclature.

### Data sources and registries

- EPA Safer Chemical Ingredients List (SCIL).
- EPA Safer Choice certified product listings.
- EPA DfE-certified disinfectant listings.
- ECHA Annex VI CLP and the C&L Inventory.
- AOEC Exposure Code List.
- EPA Endocrine Disruptor Screening Program.
- European Commission Endocrine Disruptor Assessment List.
- California Proposition 65.
- NTP, IARC, IRIS, NIOSH carcinogen and hazard lists.
- ECOSAR and EPI Suite.
- HSE COSHH Essentials.
- IFRA fragrance materials resources.
- Supplier glove performance charts and packaging supplier declarations.

### Regulatory frameworks

- FIFRA for disinfectants and pesticidal products.
- OSHA Hazard Communication Standard.
- Hazardous Air Pollutants list.
- Toxics Release Inventory.
- Montreal Protocol ozone-depleting substances restrictions.
- FTC Green Guides.
- Pollution Prevention Act and related EPA Safer Choice program authorities.
