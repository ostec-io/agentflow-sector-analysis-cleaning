# Cleaning Business & Operations Knowledge Extraction

## 1. Book Summary
This source set combines nine business, compliance, market, and ergonomics documents from BSCAI, the British Cleaning Council, Washington L&I, DFPI, and CalOSBA. Taken together, they show an industry with strong demand and persistent fragmentation: cleaning is mission-critical, labor-intensive, highly price-sensitive, and still managed with many manual, local, and inconsistent processes. The material highlights recurring commercial pressures around under-scoped contracts, workforce shortages, part-time staffing, inconsistent training, claims administration, and proof-of-service expectations. It also shows a sector beginning to formalize through workload calculators, apprenticeships, digital inspections, and more visible cleanliness standards, which makes it a strong fit for multi-agent workflow software.

## 2. Domain Problems and Pain Points
### P1. Contract pricing and workload are often misaligned
- **Problem**: Cleaning contracts are routinely priced or sold before the real workload, travel, task mix, or ergonomic burden is understood, which creates margin erosion, unsafe assignments, and disputes over what a job is really worth.
- **Who feels it**: Owners, estimators, operations managers, supervisors, frontline workers, franchisees, customers.
- **Current approach**: Manual site walks, spreadsheet estimates, ISSA baseline cleaning times, rule-of-thumb bidding, franchisor account packages, and supervisor judgment.
- **Chapter/section reference**: WA-LNI Janitorial Workload Study 2023, Executive Summary and `2. Workload Calculator Development`; WA-LNI Janitors Workload Calculator User Guide 2024, Sections `1` to `4`; DFPI *Buying a Janitorial Franchise*, `How Janitorial Services Franchises Work` and `Problems You May Face`; BSCAI *Cleaning and Disinfecting SOW Guide*, `Overview` and `Disinfection Scope of Work`.

### P2. Scope of work, frequencies, and service expectations are easy to misunderstand
- **Problem**: Customers and contractors often lack a shared, operationally precise definition of what gets cleaned, how often, at what level, and how disinfecting differs from appearance cleaning.
- **Who feels it**: Building service contractors, facility managers, occupants, inspectors, procurement teams.
- **Current approach**: Static scope-of-work documents, paper schedules, site-specific custom notes, signage, stickers, and "last cleaned by" sheets.
- **Chapter/section reference**: BSCAI *Cleaning and Disinfecting SOW Guide*, `Overview`, `Key Considerations`, and `Disinfection Scope of Work for non-health care facilities`; WA-LNI Janitors Workload Calculator User Guide 2024, `Section 2. Data Input`.

### P3. Labor shortages, part-time staffing, and an ageing workforce make delivery unstable
- **Problem**: Operators struggle to recruit and retain staff while running a workforce that is heavily part-time, older than the economy average, and frequently stretched across multiple sites and shifts.
- **Who feels it**: Cleaning business owners, supervisors, HR teams, clients, existing workers.
- **Current approach**: Overtime, managers covering shifts themselves, word-of-mouth recruiting, agency labor, and informal schedule reshuffling.
- **Chapter/section reference**: BCC 2025, `3.4 Workforce Characteristics`, `4.1 Flexible Working`, `7.1 Vacancies and Recruitment`, and `7.2 Staff Absence`; BSCAI *2024 Market Study Report*, pages `3`, `5`, and `6`.

### P4. Training is inconsistent, too informal, and not portable enough across employers
- **Problem**: Much frontline cleaning training remains on-the-job, local, and uneven, despite more complex expectations around infection control, chemicals, equipment, robotics, and team cleaning.
- **Who feels it**: New hires, supervisors, training providers, regulators, customers.
- **Current approach**: Supervisor shadowing, videos/books, informal task coaching, narrow specialist training, and emerging apprenticeship pathways.
- **Chapter/section reference**: DFPI *Buying a Janitorial Franchise*, `Training`; BCC 2025, `7.3 Internal Skills Challenge, Training And Future Skills` and `7.4 Apprenticeships In The Industry`; WA-LNI Janitorial Workload Study 2023, `3. Education and training documents`.

### P5. Injury risk is structurally tied to workload, tools, pace, and environment
- **Problem**: Janitorial work has high musculoskeletal and cardiovascular demands, and risk depends on task mix, square footage, fixtures, cleaning method, tools, recovery time, and site design.
- **Who feels it**: Janitors, employers, safety teams, regulators, workers’ compensation carriers and administrators.
- **Current approach**: Generic safety programs, post-injury claims handling, site visits, ergonomics studies, and the beta WA-LNI workload calculator.
- **Chapter/section reference**: WA-LNI Janitorial Workload Study 2023, `Executive Summary`, `Methodology`, and `2. Workload Calculator Development`; WA-LNI Janitors Workload Calculator User Guide 2024, `Background`, `Intended Use`, `Limitations`, and `Guidance on Interpreting the Evaluation Report`.

### P6. Workers’ compensation administration is operationally heavy and expensive to get wrong
- **Problem**: Employers must manage coverage decisions, classifications, premium rates, quarterly reporting, recordkeeping, audits, claims communication, return-to-work programs, and appeal deadlines.
- **Who feels it**: Owners, payroll teams, compliance teams, accountants, injured workers, claims managers.
- **Current approach**: Manual payroll records, quarterly web filing, paper notices, spreadsheet tracking, ad hoc claims follow-up, and consultant support.
- **Chapter/section reference**: WA-LNI *Employers’ Guide to Workers’ Compensation Insurance*, Chapters `2` to `7`; CalOSBA *Janitorial Business Quick Start Guide*, `For Employers`, `Worker’s Compensation`, and `Tips`.

### P7. Franchise and multi-account cleaning economics are opaque for buyers and operators
- **Problem**: Franchisees may buy account packages that do not convert into practical or profitable work once geography, timing conflicts, rejections, replacement rules, royalty fees, and lost accounts are considered.
- **Who feels it**: Prospective franchisees, franchisors, lenders, advisors, multi-site operators.
- **Current approach**: Disclosure-document review, legal/accounting advice, salesperson explanations, and simplistic revenue package comparisons.
- **Chapter/section reference**: DFPI *Buying a Janitorial Franchise*, `How Janitorial Services Franchises Work`, `Problems You May Face`, and `Protect Yourself`; BSCAI *2024 Market Study Report*, `Corporate`.

### P8. Client retention and add-on revenue are important but poorly systematized
- **Problem**: Contractors know replacement clients are expensive and that add-on services drive retention and margin, but expansion is still handled opportunistically rather than through structured account intelligence.
- **Who feels it**: Owners, sales teams, account managers, branch leaders.
- **Current approach**: Manual account reviews, acquisitions, informal upsell spotting, and service line expansion driven by management intuition.
- **Chapter/section reference**: BSCAI *2024 Market Study Report*, pages `3`, `8`, `9`, and `10`.

### P9. Sustainability, procurement, and technology proof increasingly affect who wins work
- **Problem**: Buyers increasingly ask for evidence on sustainability, employee wellbeing, healthcare-cleaning standards, robotics, smart cleaning, and supplier accreditation, but evidence collection is fragmented.
- **Who feels it**: FM providers, procurement teams, contract-cleaning bidders, suppliers, public-sector contractors.
- **Current approach**: Manual RFP narratives, supplier declarations, accreditation documents, and disconnected ESG spreadsheets.
- **Chapter/section reference**: BCC 2025, `9.3 Economic`, `9.4 Technological`, and `9.5 Environmental`.

### P10. Language, literacy, and migrant-workforce realities are not reflected in most operating systems
- **Problem**: A meaningful share of the workforce is migrant, frontline training needs to work across languages and literacy levels, and many current workflows assume strong English literacy and office-based administration.
- **Who feels it**: Frontline workers, supervisors, safety teams, training teams, community partners.
- **Current approach**: Translated PDFs, interpretation lines, informal peer coaching, and community-partner outreach.
- **Chapter/section reference**: WA-LNI Janitorial Workload Study 2023, `Education and training documents`; WA-LNI *Employers’ Guide*, `injured worker interviews`; BCC 2025, `3.4.5 Migrants`.

## 4. Workflow Patterns
### Bid-to-Contract Workloading
- **Workflow name**: Bid-to-Contract Workloading
- **Steps involved**: Intake RFP or prospect request; collect floor plans and room counts; map frequencies and task types; estimate labor and equipment needs; check workload against safe thresholds; price contract; review margin and risk; issue proposal.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Trigger from inbox or CRM creates a sprint; parallel tasks handle site extraction, workload calculation, pricing, and compliance review; BPMN approval chain routes red-flag bids to a human estimator and finance lead before proposal release.

### Scope-of-Work Authoring and Change Control
- **Workflow name**: Scope-of-Work Authoring and Change Control
- **Steps involved**: Define room types and surfaces; set frequencies; differentiate cleaning versus disinfecting; attach proof-of-service rules; publish client-approved SOW; process requested changes; roll updated tasks into operations.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: A BPMN process manages draft, review, client approval, publication, and revision; recurring tasks create operational schedules from the approved SOW; audit trails keep a version history of every scope change.

### Existing-Site Ergonomic Rebaseline
- **Workflow name**: Existing-Site Ergonomic Rebaseline
- **Steps involved**: Capture complaint, injury, or quality issue; collect current task/time/quantity data; run workload calculator or equivalent; compare against industry time standards and worker complaints; redesign task mix or staffing; validate new plan.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: A case-trigger starts a sprint with sequential data collection, workload evaluation, and redesign tasks; human review gates ensure operations and safety sign off before rollout.

### Claim-to-Return-to-Work Case Management
- **Workflow name**: Claim-to-Return-to-Work Case Management
- **Steps involved**: Log injury; gather wages, hours, and incident facts; submit claim; monitor correspondence; identify light-duty options; apply for reimbursement programs; track closures, protests, and premium impact.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: BPMN process instance tracks deadlines, forms, and case states; scheduled reminders enforce reporting windows; human approvals handle protests and appeal decisions; Sentinel alerts flag overdue employer actions.

### Daily Demand-Led Cleaning
- **Workflow name**: Daily Demand-Led Cleaning
- **Steps involved**: Read occupancy or footfall signals; detect hot zones; reprioritize tasks; notify crews; verify completion; escalate missed or failed tasks.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Event-driven triggers from IoT or service desks create micro-tasks; sequential pipelines rebalance crew plans; agent-to-agent handoffs move from monitoring to dispatch to verification.

### Multilingual Recruiting-to-Readiness
- **Workflow name**: Multilingual Recruiting-to-Readiness
- **Steps involved**: Open role; source candidates; screen for right-to-work and availability; assign language-appropriate onboarding; schedule site-specific training; verify supervisor signoff before unsupervised work.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: A sprint coordinates recruiting, document validation, translated training, and operations scheduling; knowledge graphs store worker skill, language, and site eligibility data; approval gates require supervisor readiness confirmation.

### Franchise Due Diligence and Account Validation
- **Workflow name**: Franchise Due Diligence and Account Validation
- **Steps involved**: Parse disclosure documents; test revenue package assumptions; evaluate geography and account overlap; model fees and replacement scenarios; review legal clauses; issue go/no-go memo.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Parallel analysis tasks cover legal, geographic, and unit-economics workstreams; pair sessions and handoffs reconcile conflicts; final human approval records the investment decision.

### Quarterly Reporting and Audit Readiness
- **Workflow name**: Quarterly Reporting and Audit Readiness
- **Steps involved**: Collect payroll and hours; apply risk classifications; calculate premiums and deductions; file quarterly reports; preserve records; respond to audits or discrepancy notices.
- **AgentFlow mapping**: Scheduled automation creates quarter-end filing tasks; sequential tasks validate classifications, compute premiums, and package evidence; provenance tracking keeps an audit-ready chain of payroll and filing artifacts.

## 5. Standards, APIs and Data Sources
### Industry standards and frameworks
- ISSA, *The Official ISSA Cleaning Times: The Cleaning Industry’s Most Trusted Tasks, Tools, & Workloading Resource*.
- National Standards of Healthcare Cleanliness 2021.
- Level 2 Cleaning Hygiene Operative apprenticeship.
- Level 2 Healthcare Cleaning Operative apprenticeship.
- CHSA accreditation schemes for chemical manufacturers, soft tissue, plastic refuse sacks, and cotton mops.
- Revised Strain Index / Cumulative Strain Index for hand-wrist risk.
- Recommended Cumulative Recovery Allowance for shoulder and back risk.

### Regulatory and compliance frameworks
- FTC Franchise Rule.
- Washington workers’ compensation framework under RCW and WAC.
- Washington DOSH / WISHA requirements.
- RIDDOR reporting in Great Britain.
- California workers’ compensation, wage-hour, EDD registration, SOS filing, CDTFA seller’s permit, and Proposition 65 requirements.

- UK SIC codes: `81.21`, `81.22`, `81.29`, `68.32`, `77.33`, `81.10`, `81.30`, `38.11`, `38.12`, `38.21`, `38.22`.
